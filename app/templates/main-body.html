Cunningham I: CHILDHOOD IN FOX CHASE:

St. Cecilia's Roman Catholic Church was more than a sanctuary full of pews. Its buildings -- the rectory, school, gym and the church itself -- blanketed an entire block in Fox Chase, a neighborhood so full of young Catholic families in the 1980s that an estimated 10,000 worshipers made it among the region's largest at the time.

The Rhawn Street compound, as one altar boy called it, was the center of prayer and social identity. Grade schoolers spent almost as much time there as they did at home in the heavily Irish-Catholic community.

The neighborhood was full of cops and firefighters, factory workers and secretaries, and stay-at-home moms. They raised their children with sometimes unyielding discipline, some with an unflinching devotion to the church. Being an altar boy conferred status on a family.

Cunningham's parents moved with their four children into a small twin on Lawndale Avenue in the late 1970s. His dad, James, was a shipping company worker and served as a eucharistic minister at Sunday Mass.

Obedience. Trust. Devotion to the faith. These were the values in Fox Chase. It was an ethos ripe to be exploited by a predatory priest.

The altar boy practice one afternoon turned explosive. The young priest was lording over the boys as they stood queued up in line. They knew he was militant about them hold their palms together so that fingers pointed to the sky. Interlacing fingers into a ball was akin to pointing to the devil, in the priest's eyes, and anyone who didn't get it right would suffer the consequences: Brzyski would wrap his hands around their knotted fingers until the knuckles felt like they were cracking. But on this day the priest filled them with terror.

An altar boy in line was giggling. Brzyski grabbed him and tossed him, headfirst, through the velvet curtains of a confessional.

"What is this guy doing?" Emery thought at the time. After that, he was petrified of the 6-foot-5 priest who supervised him and dozens of other altar boys.

Brzyski was just 30 years old when he was appointed associate pastor of St. Cecilia's in 1981. The son of a cop and ordained just four years earlier, he seemed relatable because he was not an elderly monsignor. But his towering 220-pound frame was imposing.

Only years later would parishioners learn that he had sexually assaulted children at his first assignment at Saint John the Evangelist in Lower Makefield – and was about to do the same with their own sons in Fox Chase.

Brzyski favored a certain type of boy. "Shy, docile, bright and intelligent and ... all physically attractive" -- and as young as 10, a grand jury would declare years later.

Brzyski also sought out children with fragile home lives or exceptionally devout parents, better to keep his sordid acts under wraps. This was a common strategy among pedophiles: They fished in troubled waters.

Delaney fit the profile. Altar boy of the year, soccer player and son of a cop. His stepfather drank. His relationship with his mother wasn't good.

"I came from a broken home," he said. "I was a shy kid, I was a little kid."

He was just 12 and weighed 80 pounds when Brzyski raped him in the rectory, he told prosecutors years later. Over the course of three years, Brzyski molested him in the grade school, the rectory, and the sacristy, where priestly robes were hung.

Gerad Argeros was the paper boy who dropped off the Bulletin every day at the rectory where Brzyski would rape him. His dad, a Teamster truck driver, and his mom, a part-time secretary, raised him and a sister in a house about a block away. With an aunt who was a nun, the church felt like home to him.

Brzyski began molesting Argeros when he was 11. Once, the priest had Argeros face-down on the rectory floor. The boy would attend religion class later and come to think his terror was God's plan.

"In the building 40 yards way from where I was raped was this mantra that God is this omnipotent, omniscient being who is steering everything that's going on," Argeros said. "And his envoy has me trapped in a room."

Jimmy Spoerl had four brothers, a sister, and strict Catholic parents – the kind who were thrilled Brzyski visited their home and showed interest in their young son. They did not know Brzyski had begun abusing Spoerl in fifth grade and was removing him from class to molest him.

Years later, Spoerl would explain why he had stayed silent about it. Once, he threatened to report Brzsyki's abuse to his parents. The priest's reply was a lie: That his mom and dad already knew -- and sanctioned it.

"He was afraid to go to school," his mother, Catherine Spoerl, said in an interview this year, "and he was afraid to come home."

Brzyski's victims did not confide in each other. Though they would later learn that their tormenter had a nickname among adults in the parish: Frisky Brzyski. One victim many years later told a grand jury that a priest walked into the sacristy once and saw Brzyski fondling the boy's naked genitals only to walk back out.

Delaney was shocked by how many others Brzyski apparently had targeted.

He was alone one day in the priest's rectory bedroom when he found a metal lock box in an armoire. Inside were photos of teen boys, many of them nude. Some were kids from St. Cecilia's.

And then, suddenly, in 1984, Brzyski disappeared from Fox Chase.

A whistleblower priest gave top church officials evidence that he had abused many boys at the prior parish in Lower Makefield. Brzyski admitted to archdiocesan officials "several acts of sexual misconduct," including with a 7th-grade boy. The church sent him to a Catholic treatment center for priests in Maryland where a top clinician said he demonstrated "pedophilia."

The archdiocese, however, did not tell parishioners.

"They said he had a mental breakdown," Catherine Spoerl recalled. "It said so in the parish bulletin."

Then-Cardinal Krol privately described the priest's actions as those of a "wolf in sheep's clothing." But no effort was made to notify law enforcement. So when Brzyski walked out of treatment and abandoned ministry in 1985, he remained a free man as the law for prosecutions expired five years later.

Not long after Bryzski left, one of Krol's top aides issued a memo ordering that no effort be made to find any victims at St. Cecilia's.

CUNNINGHAM COMES UNGLUED:

For years after the 2005 grand jury report, the archdiocese maintained it had gotten tough on abuse. Then came a 2011 grand jury's rebuke of then-Cardinal Justin Rigali. It castigated him for failing to remove credibly accused abuser priests. Rigali subsequently removed 27 clerics from active ministry.

In 2013, Cunningham lost re-election. He was in a new long-term relationship but still drinking more than he should. By November 2015, he began to come unglued.

His mentor at the jail, veteran addictions counselor Bruce Schaffer, was alarmed that his acolyte was showing up at work with the shakes from booze. Schaffer, 70, pulled him aside for a fatherly lecture.

"I need to tell you something," Cunningham said.

"Okay," Schaffer replied.

"I was molested by a priest when I was a little boy. My mother doesn't believe me."

Schaffer didn't know what to make of the second statement. But he had heard enough abuse stories through the years to conclude the first one was true.

"He didn't look at me," Schaffer remembered. "I could see the pain in his face. And there was a long pause. A long silence."

"Does that change the way you see me?" he asked Schaffer.

"No, no," Schaffer said. "Absolutely not."

Schaffer knew trauma research said sexual abuse could change the wiring in a child's brain. It often caused lifelong problems in adulthood, affecting relationships, feeding addictions, and making it hard to hold down jobs. Victims often didn't remember the actual abuse until decades later, when headlines or other stressors triggered unanticipated flashbacks. Once out, the memories could be hard to stuff back into a bottle.

Schaffer thought about probing but Cunningham was his supervisor. He instead told him to tackle one thing at a time, starting with the drinking. Schaffer and Cunningham's parents helped him into alcohol rehab.

But the abuse remained in Cunningham's thoughts. He began to talk about it to people on the periphery of his life while keeping people like his sister, Kristen, in the dark.

He gave a barebones account of his Brzyski abuse to a friend and former inmate, Sean Allen, in early 2016. Cunningham also mentioned it to Bucks County Commissioner Diane Ellis-Marseglia, a social worker and political liaison to the prison who had lost a daughter to suicide.

"He told me he had been abused by a priest," Ellis-Marseglia recalled.

In March 2016, Spoerl died after years of substance abuse. Cunningham went to the funeral. He was devastated. He began telling his parents that he, too, had been abused.

"Are you sure?" his dad would say. "Are you just saying this because you're drinking?"

BRZYSKI DALLAS

At Serendipity Apartments in Dallas, neighbors knew him only as a retired salesman with vague ties to Philadelphia. Brzyski was a mercurial neighbor, sometimes friendly, sometimes nasty. He cared for a garden of ferns and flowers, a chihuahua named Precious, and a corgi named Buddy.

He told stories. Sometimes about priests from Philadelphia. But he never said that he, too, had once been a cleric. Or that he'd been defrocked in 2004. As far as they knew, James was a retired copier-company employee with a nice nest egg -- the same thing he'd told residents at another Dallas community three years earlier before they discovered his past and ran him out.

He enjoyed free Meals on Wheels deliveries. Neighbors figured him for a highly educated man with a brazen sexual appetite for young men. He would show them pictures of naked teenagers on his computer and say they were 18 or older. He liked to "fatten up" his boys, they would hear him say time and again.

Neighbors had no knowledge of his nomadic past in Virginia, Wisconsin, California. That he had been hauled before the Philadelphia grand jury but refused to answer any questions about his alleged pedophilia. Nor did they know that he claimed during a drunk driving arrest in New Mexico that he was living only on a monthly Social Security disability check of $1,117.

On Aug. 21, a reporter and photographer from The Inquirer and Daily News knocked on the door of his ground-floor apartment. Brzyski opened it just a few inches wide, revealing a bandage on his scalp. Grey stubble coated the unshaven, battered face of a 66-year-old who looked older than his years.

Asked about St. Cecilia's he said just two words -- "Oh, no" -- and shut the door. Confronted again outside the apartment hours later, he was told that Jamie Cunningham had committed suicide and accused Brzyski of abusing him as a child. Brzyski scurried with his dogs back into his apartment and said nothing.

It was an unusual day at the complex - the day of the historic solar eclipse. Neighbors had been outside peering at the sky as Brzsyki returned to his apartment. Tenants were stunned as word immediately spread of his past.

Ten days later, on Sept. 1, Brzyski gave his dogs to an acquaintance and left his furnished apartment behind. He was seen walking away from Serendipity with only a single suitcase on wheels.

An acquaintance who spoke with him right before he left said Brzyski was downcast. "I'm either on the verge of suicide," Brzyski reportedly told the acquaintance, "or a nervous breakdown."
